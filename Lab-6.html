Question 3

mt_samples %>%
  unnest_tokens(word, transcription) %>%
  anti_join(stop_words, by = c("word")) %>%
  filter(!grepl("^\\d+$", word)) %>%
  count(word, sort = TRUE) %>%
  top_n(20, n) %>%
  ggplot(aes(n, fct_reorder(word, n))) +
  geom_col()

With stop words and numbers removed, we get a collection of more meaningful words that give us a better idea of what the text is about.

mt_samples %>%

  count(ngram, sort = TRUE) %>%
  top_n(20, n) %>%
  ggplot(aes(n, fct_reorder(ngram, n))) +
  geom_col()

Tokenizing into bi-grams yields phrases still mostly consisting of stop words. Tokenizing into tri-grams starts to give us a little more insight of what information is being recorded about patients and actions taken during procedures/surgeries. “Patient” still stands out as a very commonly used word when looking at both bi-grams and tri-grams.
